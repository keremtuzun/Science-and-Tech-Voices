Climate Technology: How Science Is Fighting Back Against a Warming Planet

Written by Kerem Tüzün • May 2026

For most of the past two centuries, human civilization ran on a simple bargain with the atmosphere. Burn carbon, get energy. The atmosphere absorbed the carbon dioxide and, for a long time, the effects seemed distant and manageable. That bargain is now coming due. The planet’s average surface temperature has risen about 1.2 degrees Celsius above pre-industrial levels. Extreme weather events are more frequent and more intense. Sea levels are rising. The science of what is happening is not seriously contested among climate researchers. But science is also one of the most powerful tools we have to respond, and over the past decade the response has been more impressive than most people realize.

The Solar Revolution

The single most dramatic energy story of the early twenty-first century is the cost collapse of solar photovoltaics. In 2010, generating a watt of solar capacity cost about four dollars. By 2024, that cost had dropped below twenty cents. This is not a gradual improvement. It is one of the steepest price declines ever recorded for any technology, steeper even than the cost decline in computer chips over the same period.

The International Energy Agency projects that solar will be the single largest source of electricity generation globally by 2030. In several countries it already is. In 2023, solar added more new power generation capacity than any other energy source for the first time in history. The reason for the collapse is manufacturing scale, supply chain maturation, and decades of materials science research that turned silicon solar cells into a commodity.

Wind Power and the Intermittency Challenge

Wind power has followed a similar trajectory. Turbines have grown taller, with longer blades capturing stronger and more consistent winds at higher altitudes. Offshore wind, anchored in shallow coastal waters or floating in deeper sea, has access to stronger and more reliable winds than onshore installations. Countries like Denmark and the UK now regularly generate more than 50 percent of their electricity from wind on good days.

The challenge that both solar and wind share is intermittency. The sun does not always shine and the wind does not always blow, but electricity demand follows its own rhythms independent of the weather. Building a grid that runs reliably on variable renewables requires either large-scale storage capacity, continent-scale transmission networks to average out regional variation, or backup power sources that can dispatch on demand.

Battery Storage: The Missing Piece

The same learning-curve dynamics that drove down solar costs are now operating in battery technology. The lithium-ion batteries that power smartphones and electric vehicles have seen cost reductions of more than 90 percent over the past fifteen years. Grid-scale battery storage installations, measured in gigawatt-hours, are being deployed at scale in the United States, China, Australia, and Europe.

For short-duration storage, covering the gap between sunset and overnight demand peaks, lithium-ion batteries are already economically competitive with natural gas peakers in many markets. Tesla’s Megapack installations, Fluence projects, and dozens of other utility-scale storage systems are operating across six continents. For longer-duration storage covering multiple cloudy or windless days, technologies including iron-air batteries, flow batteries, and compressed air storage are in development and approaching commercial deployment.

Carbon Capture: Cleaning Up the Past

Even if we stopped emitting carbon dioxide entirely today, the carbon already in the atmosphere would continue warming the planet for decades. Some scenarios for staying below 1.5 or 2 degrees of warming require not just stopping new emissions but removing carbon that has already been released.

Direct air capture machines pull carbon dioxide directly from ambient air using chemical processes and store it underground or use it to make synthetic fuels. The technology works. Climeworks in Iceland and Stratos in Texas are operating commercial direct air capture plants. The challenge is cost: current systems remove carbon dioxide at around $400 to $1,000 per ton, which is far too expensive to deploy at the scale needed on its own. But the cost has been falling, and learning curves suggest it could reach economically viable levels within this decade with sufficient investment.

Biological approaches are more immediately scalable. Restoring forests and wetlands removes carbon naturally. Changing agricultural soil management, specifically reducing tillage and adding organic matter, can turn farmland from a carbon source into a carbon sink. These approaches are imperfect because they can be reversed by fires or land use changes, but they are deployable today with existing tools.

The Role of Policy and Investment

Technology does not deploy itself. The renewable energy transition is happening partly because of falling costs and partly because of policy decisions. The US Inflation Reduction Act of 2022 committed roughly $370 billion to clean energy incentives, the largest single climate investment in American history. The EU’s Fit for 55 package set binding emissions reduction targets across member states. China, the world’s largest carbon emitter, has also announced ambitious renewable deployment goals and leads the world in solar and battery manufacturing capacity.

The interplay between technology improvement and policy support creates reinforcing feedback loops. More investment accelerates learning curves, which reduces costs, which makes clean energy economics more favorable, which attracts more investment. That feedback loop has been running for about fifteen years and shows no sign of slowing.

The Road Ahead

The world has the technical knowledge to deeply decarbonize the energy system. The resources needed are large but not beyond what economies can mobilize. What is less certain is whether the pace of deployment will be fast enough to limit warming to levels where adaptation is feasible rather than catastrophic.

The gap between what climate science says is necessary and what current policy commitments would deliver remains significant. Closing it requires sustained political will, massive capital deployment, and continued innovation across every sector of the economy. But looking at where the technology stood in 2010 and where it stands today, the case for pessimism is weaker than it was. The tools are improving. The costs are falling. The question is time, and time is exactly what we are running short of.
