AI Ethics: Can Code Have a Conscience?

Written by Kerem Tüzün • May 2026

Artificial intelligence is no longer confined to research labs and science fiction. It is embedded in the systems that decide whether you get a job interview, whether your loan application is approved, whether a healthcare algorithm flags you as high risk. Hundreds of millions of people interact with AI-driven decisions every day without knowing it. The question of whether those decisions are fair is urgent, and the technical community is only beginning to take it seriously.

Decisions Written in Numbers

Every AI system is a function. It takes input data and produces an output: a recommendation, a classification, a prediction. The function is derived from training data, historical records of past decisions and outcomes that the model learns to replicate. The problem is that the past is not neutral. Historical data reflects the biases, inequalities, and mistakes of the people who generated it. A hiring algorithm trained on a decade of successful hire records will learn to prefer candidates who resemble those past hires, which typically means it reflects the demographic biases of whoever was doing the hiring at the time. The algorithm did not create the bias, but it crystallizes and automates it at scale.

In 2018, Amazon shut down an AI recruiting tool after discovering it consistently downgraded resumes that contained the word “women’s,” as in “women’s chess club.” The model had been trained on resumes submitted to Amazon over ten years, during which most software engineers hired were men. The system learned from the past and faithfully reproduced its problems.

The Trolley Problem Gets Real

Academic ethics has debated hypothetical dilemmas like the trolley problem for decades. AI systems are now creating real versions of them. Self-driving cars need pre-programmed responses to unavoidable accident scenarios. Should a vehicle prioritize its passengers or minimize total harm? A facial recognition system used in criminal investigation has measurably different error rates across demographic groups. A medical AI might recommend more aggressive treatment for patients who are statistically similar to a group where that treatment worked well historically, even when individual circumstances suggest otherwise.

These are not distant hypotheticals. They are engineering decisions being made right now, usually without explicit acknowledgment that a value judgment is embedded in them.

Transparency and Explainability

When an algorithm denies a credit application or flags a medical image, the affected person has a legitimate interest in understanding why. In the European Union, the General Data Protection Regulation already gives individuals the right to an explanation of automated decisions that significantly affect them.

But most modern deep learning systems are not inherently explainable. A neural network with hundreds of millions of parameters processes inputs through many layers of mathematical transformations in ways that are difficult even for the engineers who built it to interpret clearly. The field of explainable AI is working on tools to address this, from attention visualization to counterfactual explanations, but it remains an active and unsolved technical challenge.

Accountability: Who Is Responsible?

When an AI system causes harm, assigning responsibility is harder than it might seem. Is it the company that deployed the system? The data providers whose records trained it? The developers who wrote the objective function? The regulators who approved it? Current legal frameworks were not designed with algorithmic harm in mind, and there is genuine disagreement about how liability should be distributed.

Several governments have moved to address this gap. The EU’s AI Act, passed in 2024, is the first comprehensive AI regulation in the world. It classifies AI systems by risk level, requires transparency and human oversight for high-risk applications in hiring, credit, healthcare, and law enforcement, and bans certain uses outright, including social scoring by governments and most real-time biometric surveillance in public spaces.

Fairness Cannot Be Defined Once

One technical complication in AI fairness is that there is no single definition of fairness that satisfies all intuitions simultaneously. A system can be equally accurate on average across groups while still producing different error rates for different groups. A system can have equal false positive rates while having unequal predictive accuracy. Researchers have shown mathematically that in most real situations, you cannot satisfy all common definitions of fairness at once.

This means that every AI system making consequential decisions embeds a value judgment about which kind of fairness matters most. That judgment should be made explicitly and defensibly, with input from the people affected, not left implicit in an optimization formula written by engineers who may not share the circumstances of those being classified.

The Role of Diversity in AI Development

The demographics of the AI research community are heavily skewed. A 2021 Stanford survey found that women represent about 16 percent of tenured faculty in AI research. Representation of Black and Hispanic researchers is lower still. This matters because the people building AI systems bring their own blind spots to the work, and diverse teams are more likely to notice problems that affect groups the team itself does not include.

Several major AI labs have expanded their ethics and fairness research teams significantly over the past five years. Researchers like Joy Buolamwini at MIT and Timnit Gebru, who co-founded the DAIR Institute, have been influential in documenting bias in commercial AI systems and pushing for better standards.

A Human-Centered Future

The technical tools for building fairer AI systems are improving: better auditing methods, more diverse datasets, interpretability research, formal fairness constraints that can be included in training objectives. These are engineering problems, and they are tractable given sufficient effort.

But technical solutions alone are not enough. The systems that matter most are deployed in contexts of significant power imbalance, between an employer and a job applicant, between a bank and a borrower, between a government and a citizen. Accountability in those contexts requires not just better algorithms but better oversight institutions, clearer legal standards, and genuine input from the people most affected by automated decisions.

Code may not have a conscience. But the people writing and deploying code do. The challenge of AI ethics is not a technical puzzle to be solved once and forgotten. It is an ongoing responsibility that accompanies every decision to automate something that matters.
