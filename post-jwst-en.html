The James Webb Space Telescope: A Window to the Dawn of Time

Written by Kerem Tüzün • May 2026

On Christmas Day 2021, a rocket lifted off from Kourou, French Guiana, carrying what may be the single most complex science instrument ever launched into space. The James Webb Space Telescope had been in development for 25 years, at a total cost of about $10 billion, and the entire astronomical community held its breath. Everything had to work perfectly. There was no way to repair it once it was in position, 1.5 million kilometers from Earth. It worked. And what it has shown us in its first three years of operation has surpassed what most scientists dared to expect.

Why Build a New Space Telescope

The Hubble Space Telescope, launched in 1990, changed how we see the universe. It extended our view back to galaxies forming a few hundred million years after the Big Bang and produced images of depth and clarity that no ground-based telescope could match. But Hubble primarily observes in visible light and ultraviolet. The most distant objects in the universe, the first galaxies that formed after the Big Bang, have their light stretched by the expansion of space into the infrared range by the time it reaches us. To see them clearly, you need a telescope designed for infrared, and Hubble was not.

Webb was. Its primary mirror is 6.5 meters across, nearly three times the area of Hubble’s 2.4 meter mirror. It observes primarily in near and mid-infrared wavelengths. To prevent its own heat from drowning out the faint infrared signals from distant objects, it operates at minus 233 degrees Celsius, just 40 degrees above absolute zero, shielded from the Sun and Earth by a five-layer sunshield the size of a tennis court.

Getting Into Position: L2

Webb was too large to fit in any rocket in its final configuration. It had to be folded like origami, launched, and then unfolded in space over two weeks, with 344 individual deployments that all had to succeed in sequence. Engineers had identified every possible point of failure and tested everything exhaustively over years of preparation. It still took tremendous discipline to watch it unfold.

Webb was sent to the second Lagrange point, L2, a location 1.5 million kilometers from Earth in the direction away from the Sun. At L2, the gravitational pulls of the Earth and Sun combine with the centrifugal force of orbit to keep the telescope in a stable position. The sunshield blocks the heat and light from both Sun and Earth permanently, allowing Webb to maintain the extraordinary cold it needs to operate.

The First Images

In July 2022, NASA released Webb’s first science images. They showed a galaxy cluster called SMACS 0723, with thousands of background galaxies visible behind it, some so distant that the light had traveled more than 13 billion years to reach the telescope. Objects that Hubble had resolved only as faint smudges were now sharp, with visible structure, color, and form.

Images of the Carina Nebula showed the region where new stars are forming in detail never seen before, with pillars of gas and dust sculpted by stellar winds visible at scales that rewrote what we knew about star formation. Images of the Southern Ring Nebula resolved both stars at the center of the dying stellar remnant for the first time. The scientific community had expected excellent images. The quality was beyond what most had dared to expect.

Studying the Early Universe

Webb was designed primarily to study the era when the first stars and galaxies formed, roughly 200 to 400 million years after the Big Bang. Within its first year of operation, Webb identified galaxy candidates at distances corresponding to formation within 300 million years of the Big Bang. Some of these early galaxies are significantly more massive than theoretical models predicted, which has prompted substantial revision of galaxy formation models.

The telescope has also observed the epoch of reionization, the period when light from the first stars gradually burned through the opaque neutral hydrogen gas filling the early universe and made it transparent for the first time. Understanding when and how this transition happened is one of the major open questions in cosmology, and Webb is now producing data that lets researchers probe it directly.

Exoplanet Atmospheres

Webb was not designed exclusively to look at distant galaxies. When a planet passes in front of its star, some of the starlight passes through the planet’s atmosphere. Different molecules absorb different wavelengths of infrared light, leaving chemical fingerprints in the spectrum. Webb can detect those fingerprints with far greater sensitivity than any previous telescope.

In 2022, Webb detected carbon dioxide in the atmosphere of a gas giant called WASP-39b, the first direct detection of carbon dioxide in an exoplanet atmosphere. In 2023, Webb detected dimethyl sulfide in the atmosphere of K2-18b, a planet about 8.6 times the mass of Earth in the habitable zone of its star. Dimethyl sulfide on Earth is produced only by living organisms, though scientists were cautious about calling the detection a biosignature, since abiotic sources are possible.

What Comes Next

Webb has enough fuel to operate for at least twenty years. The scientific program ahead is enormous. The telescope will continue surveying the earliest galaxies, mapping the large-scale structure of the universe, and analyzing the atmospheric chemistry of dozens of exoplanets. Rocky planets in the habitable zones of nearby small stars are among the highest-priority targets for future atmospheric observations.

Every image Webb produces is a window that was previously closed. The universe it has revealed is bigger, older, and more surprising than the one we thought we understood before it launched. The first three years of data have already prompted revisions to multiple areas of cosmological theory, and there are nineteen more years of fuel remaining. What we will discover with it is genuinely unpredictable.
