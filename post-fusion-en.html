Fusion Energy: The Promise of Unlimited Clean Power

Written by Kerem Tüzün • May 2026

On December 5, 2022, scientists at the National Ignition Facility in Livermore, California, fired 192 laser beams simultaneously at a capsule about the size of a peppercorn filled with hydrogen isotopes. The capsule compressed and heated so rapidly that nuclear fusion occurred. For the first time in the history of controlled fusion research, the energy produced by the reaction exceeded the energy delivered to the fuel target. The milestone was historic. But it was also, in important ways, only a beginning.

What Fusion Is

Nuclear fusion is the process that powers the Sun and every other star. It forces light atomic nuclei together under extreme temperature and pressure until they merge, forming a heavier nucleus and releasing a large amount of energy. The energy comes from the fact that the resulting nucleus is slightly lighter than the sum of its parts, and that missing mass is converted directly to energy.

The most practical fusion reaction for energy generation uses deuterium and tritium, two hydrogen isotopes. Deuterium is abundant in seawater. Tritium can be produced from lithium, which is widely available. A fusion power plant would produce no carbon dioxide, generate far less radioactive waste than fission reactors, cannot melt down in the way fission reactors can, and could run on fuels extracted from the ocean essentially indefinitely.

Why It Has Been So Hard

The conditions required for fusion are extreme. To get hydrogen nuclei close enough to fuse, they need to be heated to about 100 million degrees Celsius, roughly seven times hotter than the core of the Sun. At that temperature, matter exists as plasma, a superheated cloud of free nuclei and electrons. Containing that plasma so that it does not touch the walls of the reactor and immediately cool down has been the central engineering challenge for seven decades.

The joke in the fusion community is that practical fusion power is always thirty years away. It has been that joke for sixty years. The difficulty is real. Containing a plasma at 100 million degrees requires magnetic fields of extraordinary strength and precision. Tiny instabilities in the field can cause the plasma to touch the reactor wall, quenching the reaction instantly. Building materials must withstand neutron bombardment without becoming brittle. Superconducting magnets must be cooled to near absolute zero while the plasma a few meters away is at temperatures far hotter than the Sun’s core.

ITER: The International Bet

The most ambitious response to the fusion challenge is ITER, the International Thermonuclear Experimental Reactor under construction in southern France. ITER is a collaboration between 35 countries including the United States, the European Union, China, Russia, India, South Korea, and Japan. Its construction cost has been estimated at around 20 billion euros, making it one of the most expensive scientific projects in history.

ITER is designed to demonstrate scientific viability, not commercial power generation. It aims to produce ten times as much fusion energy as the heating power delivered to the plasma. But it will not convert that energy to electricity and is not designed for continuous operation. It is a proof of concept at the scale needed to validate the technologies required for a real power plant. First plasma is expected in 2025, with full deuterium-tritium operations in the early 2030s.

Private Companies and the Race to Ignition

While ITER has proceeded on a multi-decade government timeline, private investment in fusion has accelerated dramatically. Commonwealth Fusion Systems, a spinout from MIT, raised $1.8 billion to build a compact tokamak using high-temperature superconducting magnets far more powerful than those available when ITER was designed. The new magnets allow a much smaller reactor to achieve the same plasma conditions, potentially reducing cost by orders of magnitude. Their SPARC prototype is targeting first plasma around 2025.

Helion Energy raised $500 million from investors including Sam Altman and signed a contract with Microsoft to deliver fusion electricity to the grid by 2028. TAE Technologies, General Fusion, and more than two dozen other private companies are pursuing different approaches, from tokamaks and stellarators to inertial confinement and field-reversed configurations. In 2021 and 2022 alone, private fusion companies raised more than $3 billion, a sign that serious investors believe the timeline has shortened.

What the NIF Result Actually Means

The December 2022 National Ignition Facility result should be understood carefully. The facility delivered 2.05 megajoules to the target capsule and got 3.15 megajoules of fusion energy out, a true gain in terms of energy delivered to the fuel. However, the lasers that produced those 2.05 megajoules consumed about 300 megajoules of electricity from the grid. The system-level energy balance was still deeply negative.

NIF uses inertial confinement, blasting the fuel from all sides simultaneously with lasers, rather than the magnetic confinement used by tokamaks and most private companies. Its laser-based approach is probably not the path to a commercial power plant. But the result mattered because it demonstrated that fusion ignition is physically achievable in a controlled laboratory setting, removing a remaining scientific question and validating that the underlying physics works.

What Fusion Energy Could Mean

A commercially viable fusion power plant would be transformative. It would produce large amounts of baseload electricity without carbon emissions, using fuels available from seawater and lithium deposits around the world. It would not depend on intermittent resources like solar and wind. It would generate radioactive waste, but waste that becomes safe within decades rather than the tens of thousands of years required for some fission byproducts. And unlike fission reactors, it cannot run away: if you stop feeding it fuel, it stops.

The uncertainty is timeline and cost. Even optimistic estimates from private companies put grid-connected fusion in the late 2030s at the earliest. Most serious analysts expect the 2040s or beyond for fusion to contribute meaningfully to global electricity supply. But the fact that serious investors are now betting billions on fusion timelines that only physicists discussed optimistically a decade ago is itself a signal. The Sun has been running on fusion for five billion years. Getting a controlled version of that process to run reliably on Earth would be one of the greatest engineering achievements in human history.
