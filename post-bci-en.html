Brain-Computer Interfaces: When Humans and Machines Think Together

Written by Kerem Tüzün • May 2026

In 2021, a man named Philip O’Keefe sent a tweet. That might not sound remarkable on its own, but Philip had ALS, a degenerative disease that had paralyzed him completely. He composed the tweet using only his thoughts, by imagining cursor movement and selecting letters, while a chip implanted in his motor cortex recorded his neural activity and translated it into commands. That moment marked something genuinely new in the relationship between the human brain and digital systems. Brain-computer interfaces have moved from theoretical curiosity to clinical reality in the span of a few years, and the ambitions behind them have grown to match.

How a Brain-Computer Interface Works

The brain communicates through electrical signals. Neurons fire in patterns that correspond to thoughts, intentions, and perceptions. A BCI captures some of those signals, processes them, and converts them into digital instructions. The simplest interfaces are non-invasive: electrodes placed on the scalp can detect broad electrical patterns well enough to let someone control a cursor on a screen. More precise systems require placing electrodes directly on or inside the brain.

The current clinical standard for high-resolution recording is an implant called the Utah Array, a grid of 96 thin electrodes inserted into the motor cortex. It can record the firing of individual neurons with enough fidelity to translate intended hand movements into digital commands in real time. This is how O’Keefe sent his tweet. Researchers at BrainGate, a consortium of academic institutions, have used similar systems to help paralyzed patients type at speeds approaching normal handwriting.

Medical Applications: Restoring What Was Lost

The most immediate and least controversial application of BCIs is in medicine. Patients with ALS, spinal cord injuries, stroke-related paralysis, and locked-in syndrome have the most to gain. In clinical trials, BCI systems have restored the ability to communicate for people who had lost it entirely. High-density electrode arrays have allowed patients to type at 40 to 90 characters per minute using imagined handwriting.

A 2023 study from Stanford published in Nature used a non-invasive BCI combined with a speech neuroprosthesis to give a woman who had been unable to speak for 18 years a synthesized voice that expressed her words in near-real time, matching her intended speech with 78 percent accuracy without any decoder retraining. Beyond communication, experimental systems at Case Western Reserve University have allowed patients with cervical spinal injuries to regrasp objects using their own muscles, stimulated by decoded neural commands.

Neuralink and the Commercial Race

In 2024, Neuralink received FDA approval and implanted its device in the first human patient. The company’s implant is a coin-sized chip housing 1,024 flexible electrodes, significantly more than previous clinical systems, with a wireless transmitter that eliminates the need for a physical cable through the skull. The first patient, Noland Arbaugh, demonstrated the ability to control a computer cursor and play video games using only his thoughts within weeks of the procedure.

Other companies are working on less invasive approaches. Synchron has developed a device called the Stentrode that is threaded into the brain through blood vessels rather than open surgery, eliminating the need to cut through the skull. Precision Neuroscience places a flexible electrode array on the brain’s surface without penetrating it. Each approach makes different tradeoffs between signal quality and surgical risk.

The Ethics of Writing to the Brain

Most BCIs today read from the brain. They decode neural signals but do not alter them. The next generation of devices, already in development, will write to the brain as well, delivering precisely targeted stimulation to alter perception, mood, memory consolidation, or attention. Deep brain stimulation already does this for Parkinson’s disease. Researchers have shown that stimulating the hippocampus at specific moments can improve memory consolidation in patients with memory disorders.

The ethical questions this raises are serious. If a device can modulate mood or focus, who controls the settings? If an employer could offer workers a productivity implant, would the pressure to accept it be coercive? If neural data reveals intentions before you act on them, who owns that information and what are they allowed to do with it? These questions need answers before the technology scales, and the frameworks for addressing them barely exist yet.

Privacy and Neural Data

Neural data is unlike any other kind of personal data. Your brain activity contains not just thoughts you have expressed but thoughts you have not, emotional reactions you may not be consciously aware of, and the raw substrate of your inner life. No current legal framework adequately protects this data. A company that builds a BCI and processes the data it generates could theoretically infer far more about the user than the user intended to share.

Colorado became the first US state to explicitly extend privacy protections to neural data in 2024. The Neurorights Foundation, founded by neuroscientist Rafael Yuste at Columbia University, has been working with several countries to add neurorights to existing human rights frameworks. Chile added neurorights to its constitution in 2021, the first country to do so. The international conversation is only beginning.

Looking Forward

The near-term future of BCIs is in medicine. More patients with paralysis, locked-in syndrome, epilepsy, and treatment-resistant depression will benefit from improving systems. Signal quality, miniaturization, and the biocompatibility of electrode materials are all advancing rapidly. Wireless systems with battery charging through the skin are eliminating the last physical connections between brain and device.

What comes after that depends partly on technology and partly on the societal decisions we make about what these tools should be allowed to become. The brain is where we draw the line between the self and the world. Brain-computer interfaces are moving that line. Where it ends up depends on choices being made right now, in engineering labs, regulatory agencies, and ethical debates, by people who are only beginning to understand what they are building.
